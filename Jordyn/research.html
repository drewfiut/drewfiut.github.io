"The Effects of Academic Major on Reaction Time Using a Change Blindness Paradigm"

The failure of visual detection of an environmental change by observers is known as change blindness (Schankin, Bergmann, Schubert, & Hagemann, 2017). Schankin et. al. (2017) further states that attention is controlled by top-down processing. It is assumed that forensic science students have more top-down knowledge (i.e., experience, expectation, and context). Forensic science students are trained to be aware of their surroundings, as opposed to other majors focusing on more theoretic domains. The current study used a change blindness flicker paradigm (Rensink, Oâ€™Regan & Clark, 1997) to see if Hilbert College forensic science students react more quickly to environmental change compared to Hilbert College students in majors such as Business or English. Therefore, it was expected that faster reaction times would be seen in forensic science majors as opposed to business majors in a change blindness paradigm.
